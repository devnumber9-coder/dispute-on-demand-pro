What Credit Bureaus Must Do

Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA), credit bureaus are required to:

  • Investigate disputes within 30 days of receiving your dispute letter (may be extended to 45 days in some cases)
  • Correct or delete inaccurate, incomplete, or unverifiable information
  • Provide you with results of their investigation in writing
  • Notify the furnisher (the company that provided the information) of the dispute
  • Provide a free updated credit report if information is changed or deleted

What You Can Do Yourself (For Free)

You Don't Need to Pay Anyone to Dispute Credit Report Errors

Federal law gives you the right to dispute credit report errors directly with the credit bureaus at no cost. Here's how:

  1. Get your credit reports from www.annualcreditreport.com (federally authorized, 100% free)
  2. Review each report carefully for errors, inaccuracies, or outdated information
  3. Write a dispute letter to each credit bureau with errors (certified mail, return receipt requested)
  4. Include documentation supporting your dispute (copies, not originals)
  5. Wait for investigation (30-45 days)
  6. Review results and follow up if necessary

Total Cost: $0 (except postage for certified mail, approximately $8-10 per letter)

Accurate Negative Information Cannot Be Removed

Important: Credit bureaus are legally required to report accurate information, even if it's negative. You generally cannot remove:

  • Late payments that actually occurred
  • Bankruptcies (7-10 years)
  • Foreclosures (7 years)
  • Collections (7 years from date of first delinquency)
  • Tax liens (indefinite if unpaid; 7 years if paid)
  • Valid judgments (7 years or until statute of limitations expires)

You CAN dispute:

  • Information that is factually incorrect
  • Accounts that don't belong to you (identity theft)
  • Duplicate accounts
  • Information reported beyond the legal time limit
  • Outdated information
  • Information the credit bureau cannot verify

State-Specific Credit Repair Laws

In addition to federal law, many states have their own credit repair laws with additional protections. Some states require:

  • Registration or bonding of credit repair organizations
  • Longer cancellation periods (e.g., 5 days in some states)
  • Additional disclosure requirements
  • Prohibition of upfront fees before services are rendered

States with additional credit repair laws include: California, Colorado, Connecticut, Florida, Illinois, Louisiana, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, Nevada, New Hampshire, New York, North Carolina, Oklahoma, Oregon, Tennessee, Texas, Utah, Virginia, Washington, and others.

Dispute On Demand provides state-specific information within the app to help you understand your rights based on your location.

Penalties for Violations

Credit repair organizations that violate the CROA may face:

  • Civil penalties up to $5,000 per violation
  • Consumer lawsuits for actual damages
  • Punitive damages if violations were willful
  • Attorney's fees and court costs
